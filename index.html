Health on the Line: 6 Miles and 23 Years Along the 66 Bus Route

Boston is a deeply unequal city. Despite being one of the richest cities in America, with a median income of nearly $100,000, it is also one of the most unequal. Boston has the highest income inequality of any city in the nation.1 If Boston were its own country, its GINI coefficient for income inequality2 would put it on par with Colombia, and behind only South Africa and Namibia, the two most unequal countries in the world.3

This inequality is reflected in health outcomes. While Boston has the best healthcare of any city in the world,4 and the state has the country's second-highest life expectancy,5 Bostonians face starkly different health outlooks depending on where they live. A few miles can make the difference of decades in median life expectancy — from reaching one's 60s to reaching ones's 90s.6

Greater Boston's transit infrastructure is itself a reflection of the city's deep divides. All the major rail lines connect the city's hub to its wealthy suburbs, mostly bypassing the poorer parts of the city — Roxbury, Dorchester, Mattapan, Chelsea, Everett, and Revere. This arterial rail layout leaves the 66 bus route the closest thing Greater Boston has to a ring route, connecting with all branches of the Green Line, the Red Line, and the Orange Line, and running through dramatically unequal neighborhoods in just six miles.

Route 66 begins in Cambridge's Harvard Square, where Johnston Gate opens onto Harvard University, the nation's oldest and arguably most prestigious institution of higher learning. It then crosses the Charles River into the Boston neighborhood of Allston, where Harvard owns a third of all property.7 The bus then continues into Brighton, a part of the city that historically has been much poorer than its neighbors. From there, the 66 crosses into Brookline, the closest of the highly affluent Boston suburbs. Exiting Brookline, the bus passes by the Longwood Medical Area, home to eight hospitals and Harvard's Medical, Dental, and Public Health Schools. The bus ends its journey in Roxbury, one of the poorest areas in the city, home to the so-called "Methadone Mile."

These factors make the 66 bus the perfect vehicle for understanding the social determinants of health in Greater Boston. Decades of redlining and systematic neglect established and solidified the stark disparities visible along Route 66: unequal access to housing, greenspace, a clean environment, healthcare, and public investment in infrastructure and services. A social medicine perspective allows us to understand how our built environment can create inequalities, causing vastly different health outcomes for individuals depending on where they live.

This project interweaves statistics, photography, and narrative to shed light on these inequalities. It invites you as the viewer to viscerally experience what can often be out of sight out of mind for many Boston-area residents. None of these disparities is an accident. They are the product of decades of discriminatory policies. That doesn’t mean it must always be this way. Bad policies can be undone.
